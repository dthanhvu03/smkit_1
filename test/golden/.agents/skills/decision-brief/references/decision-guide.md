# Decision framing reference — turn a fuzzy request into a fundable decision

Loaded on demand by the `decision-brief` skill (progressive disclosure). Compiled from the
frameworks listed at the end. Written for a non-technical founder.

## 1. Frame the problem, not the solution
A request usually names a *solution the founder guessed at* ("add Google login"). Restate
it as the underlying **problem/goal** ("reduce signup friction so more visitors finish
registering"). Solving the stated solution when the real problem is elsewhere is the most
expensive mistake — and the cheapest to avoid.

## 2. Jobs-to-be-Done — who "hires" this, and for what?
People don't want a feature; they hire it to make progress in a situation. Ask: *who* is
in *what situation*, and *what progress* are they trying to make? This surfaces the real
success criterion and often a simpler solution than the one requested.

## 3. Options, always more than one
List **at least two or three** ways to reach the goal — including the **cheapest / manual**
one and **"do nothing."** Each with an honest trade-off. The smallest option that still
teaches you whether it's worth building usually wins.

## 4. Smallest slice that teaches the most (MVP thinking)
Don't build the whole thing. Build the **minimum** that validates the riskiest assumption
with real users, then decide based on what you learn. Favor learning over completeness.

## 5. Reversible vs irreversible (two-way vs one-way doors)
- **Two-way door** (easily reversible) — decide fast, cheaply; you can undo it. Most
  decisions are these.
- **One-way door** (hard/impossible to reverse — data model, a public API, deleting data,
  a legal/financial commitment) — slow down, get it right, and get sign-off.
Match the ceremony to the door: don't agonize over reversible calls; don't rush
irreversible ones.

## 6. Cost & risk — labelled estimates, and cost of delay
Give a **rough, explicitly-labelled** effort/cost estimate (never a guess dressed as a
fact) and name the **risk most likely to make it a waste** (no users, a hidden dependency,
legal/data). Consider the **cost of delay** — what is lost each week it isn't done — as
well as the cost to build.

## 7. The founder's decision
Isolate the **one or two questions only the founder can answer** (budget, audience,
acceptable risk) and ask them in plain language. End with a **recommendation** and a
one-sentence definition of "done" they would accept.
